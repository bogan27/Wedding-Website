From the U.S.

Remember - you need a passport! Make sure yours is valid, and don't wait to renew.

From New England: Aer Lingus is the least expensive airline to offer multiple daily direct flights from Logan Airport to Dublin. In some cases, you can get even better fares flying Norwegian Air out of Providence.

From Philadelphia: Aer Lingus is the least expensive airline flying direct from Philadelphia to Dublin, though there is only one daily option. Finnair is a reasonably priced alternative.


From Europe

Getting to Dublin: Aer Lingus and Ryanair cover most destinations in Europe. Ryanair tends to be the cheapest.

Getting Around Ireland

Car Rental: The wedding venue, Tulfarris, is outside of the city, so you will likely want to rent a car if you are coming from abroad. You can rent a car at Dublin Airport, which offers many of the same rental companies as those found in the U.S. (Avis, Budget, Enterprise, and Hertz) as well as several European companies.
Note that in Ireland, there are stricter drunk driving laws, and you drive on the left side of the road (opposite the U.S.).

Taxis and Public Transit: Uber and Lyft aren't as mainstream as in the U.S. MyTaxi is an option for hailing taxis from your phone. There is also public transit via busses, trains, and trolleys for travel in and arround Dublin.

Attractions

Powerscourt Estate and gardens

1 hour drive from Tulfarris, 40 mins from Dublin

Powerscourt Estate, one of Ireland's leading tourism attractions featuring Powerscourt House & Gardens and Two Championship Golf Courses. Come for a walk around and grab lunch at avoca in the centre. Avoca, an Irish, family-run business that is one of the oldest surviving manufacturers in the world, home to some of Ireland’s best-loved cafés and retail. Avoca boasts assortments of fresh salads, and the best brown bread and desserts.

Sally’s Gap

On route from Dublin to Tulfarris, if avoiding M50

Take the scenic road to Tulfarris and drive on the Sally’s Gap road. On the road to Sally Gap, there are spectacular views of the surrounding blanket bog and the Wicklow Mountains. Sally Gap is one of two east-to-west passes across the Wicklow Mountains. Sally Gaps is a cross-road that leads you North to Dublin, West to Blessington, South to Glendalough or East to Roundwood. the Sally Gap got its road after the Irish rebellion of 1798. It was built by British Army forces looking to flush rebels from the hills, and to this day is known as the Military Road. Whatever about giving the army a better view of the rebels, the Military Road certainly provides an enviable view of some of Ireland’s most filmed scenery. Highlights of this winding, twisting feat of engineering include the Glencree valley, the dark waters of Lough Tay, Kippure Mountain and Glenmacnass Waterfall.

Glendalough

30 min drive from Tullfaris, 50 mins from Dublin

Glendalough is home to one of the most important monastic sites in Ireland. This early Christian monastic settlement was founded by St. Kevin in the 6th century and from this developed the “Monastic City”. Most of the buildings that survive today date from the 10th through 12th centuries. Despite attacks by Vikings over the years, Glendalough thrived as one of Ireland’s great ecclesiastical foundations and schools of learning until the Normans destroyed the monastery in 1214 A.D. and the dioceses of Glendalough and Dublin were united. Glendalough also boasts some breathtaking walks with very scenic views. So get yours walking shoes on and check it out.

  • Taste some local drinks with a tour of the Guinness Factory or Jameson distillery.
  • Head to Temple Bar to hear some local live music while enjoying these local beverages further.
  • Uncover some of Dublin’s history with a tour of Dublin Castle or Kilmainham Gaol.
  • Head south to the sea to visit Dun Laoghaire, Dalkey and Killiney Castle for some scenic walks and famous ice cream.
  • Head north to the sea to visit Howth with great seafood and a scenic cliff walk.
